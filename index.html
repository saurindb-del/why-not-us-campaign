Rethink

What we've been told vs. what's actually true.

The beliefs holding us back aren't facts — they're outdated scripts. Here's what the research actually says.

Myth

"Real men don't need therapy."

Boys are taught from a young age to hide their feelings and deal with problems alone. This creates an internal belief that asking for help is a sign of weakness or failure.

Truth

Therapy builds strength, not weakness.

Research shows men respond positively to therapy when it's framed as building coping skills and emotional resilience — the same way athletes train their bodies (APA).

Myth

"If I go to therapy, people will think something's wrong with me."

Teens worry about peer judgment more than almost anything. The fear of being labeled or treated differently keeps many from ever reaching out.

Truth

Talking about it is the bravest thing you can do.

Public figures and athletes who speak openly about therapy are helping normalize it. Visibility breaks stigma. The more we see it, the more normal it becomes.

Myth

"I should be able to handle this on my own."

Media often portrays strong male characters as emotionless, independent, and never asking for help. These portrayals set unrealistic expectations for real life.

Truth

Even the strongest people have a team behind them.

No one succeeds alone. Therapy is a tool — like a coach, a mentor, or a training plan. It helps you perform better in every part of life.

Real Talk

Sound familiar? Let's rethink it.

These are real situations where outdated thinking holds us back. There's a better way.

💬

Your friend hasn't been himself lately.

"He'll figure it out. I don't want to make it weird."

"Hey, I've noticed you seem off. I'm here if you want to talk. No judgment."

💪

Someone mentions they're in therapy.

"That's kinda weird. He must really be going through it."

"Respect. Taking care of your mind takes guts."

🧠

You've been feeling stressed and overwhelmed.

"I just need to push through it. Talking about it won't help."

"Maybe I should talk to someone who can help me build better strategies."

🏆

A pro athlete talks about mental health publicly.

"He's soft. Athletes shouldn't be talking about that stuff."

"If someone at the top of their game uses therapy, maybe it actually works."

Take Action

What you can do right now.

You don't need a degree or a platform. Change starts in your conversations, your group chats, and your locker rooms.

1

Check In

Ask your friends how they're really doing. A simple "you good?" can open a door someone's been afraid to walk through.

2

Watch Your Words

Stop using mental health as a punchline. Phrases like "that's so OCD" or calling someone "psycho" reinforce harmful stigma.

3

Normalize It

Talk about therapy the same way you'd talk about going to the gym. The more casual we make it, the less scary it becomes.

4

Lead by Example

If you've talked to someone or gotten help, share that. Your honesty gives others permission to do the same.

5

Challenge Stereotypes

When someone says "man up" or "boys don't cry," push back. Strength isn't silence — it's knowing when to speak up.

6

Share This Campaign

Send this to a friend, post it, screenshot it. The more people who see this, the more the conversation grows.
